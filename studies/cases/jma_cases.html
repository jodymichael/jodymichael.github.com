Case Studies :: Career Development

Alexis: What do I want to be when I grow up?

Situation:
Alexis was a student in her first semester of college when her father strongly suggested she visit Jody Michael & Associates [JMA]. He worried that she had already chosen a college curriculum and vocation that didn't meet her needs: teaching. Luckily, Alexis's father had not asked JMA to change Alexis's mind. However, he felt that JMA's experience and separation from the situation would lead to a better chance of our helping his daughter think through her career choice than he had.

Resolution:
Alexis was sent to us by her father. As such, our first priority was establishing trust. The casual manner in which Jody Michael conducted the session, as well as a guarantee of confidentiality no matter who paid the bill, set Alexis at ease.

Alexis had decided to pursue a degree in education so that she could take a job as a K-12 teacher after graduation. She enjoyed helping people and wanted a career that would be different, day-to-day. She had an aversion to math, but most importantly, she feared making the wrong choice. Alexis worried that she would be unable to support herself as an adult. She also figured that the need for teachers would continue indefinitely. Therefore, her choice was safe.

Jody then changed the nature of the game by asking, "What career would you choose if I guaranteed you a job after graduation?" With this question, Alexis revealed that she was fascinated by other means of helping people: psychology, criminology, and counseling. 'Psychologist' was actually her dream job. It allowed the practitioner self-direction, and made use of one-to-one interaction. However, psychology also entailed a focus on research and assessment she had little interest in. It required a graduate degree, a commitment she was unwilling to make. Lastly, she was wary of the job market.

Teaching, it turned out, was a backup plan, a route she'd decided upon because she was unsure of the future market for psychologists. Unfortunately, Alexis' backup plan was not ideal. It was a 'helping' profession, but certainly, it was repetitious from year to year, dealt with the same kids day-to-day, and frequently, the same subject lesson, from one hour to the next. Furthermore, it promised little autonomy and required group leadership, a far cry from the intimate and personal discussion that was possible one-on-one. On the positive side, however, Alexis recognized the three advantages every teacher looks forward to: June, July, and August.

Having fleshed out which parts of Alexis' decision process were determined by wants, needs, and fears, Jody suggested she consider social work. Alexis had not considered this field seriously because she had limited information about the profession.

Jody has coached people from many professions and industries. Without additional research, she told Alexis what she knew of social work. No two clients were the same. Unlike psychology, social work did not require the professional to administer a battery of tests and assessments. The social worker-patient interaction was realMost importantly, school social workers are not only in demand, but the job includes the three month vacation that teachers get and Alexis coveted. In this way, Alexis realized that the safe fall-back selection of 'teacher' was not a good fit for her. If she succeeded in achieving this goal, she would be dissatisfied. On the other hand, school social work satisfied her passion for psychology and had the benefits of teaching but included none of the related downsides. Alexis left the appointment with Jody Michael excited to work toward her new career, confident that it was something she would enjoy doing.

Outcome:
Alexis, after one extended session, had chosen a career that suited her needs. Fear no longer drove her decisions. Most important: Alexis was excited to start on this new adventure. After the session, she returned to the college registrar's office to change her major and get into the right classes.
